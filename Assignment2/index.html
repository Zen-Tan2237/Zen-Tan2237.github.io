The Singapore MRT

Introduction

Key Milestones

Early Beginnings

  • 1967: Planning for the MRT system begins.
  • Oct 1983: Start of construction.

Initial Opening

  • 7 Nov 1987: MRT system officially opens from Yio Chu Kang to Toa Payoh
  • 6 Jul 1990: Initial planned system of the MRT system opens.
    (consisting of the North South Line, "Branch Line (consisting of Jurong East to Chua Chu Kang)" and East West Line)
  • 10 Feb 1996: Woodlands extension from Yishun to Chua Chu Kang opens, taking over the "Branch Line"

The system expands...

  • 6 Nov 1999: Bukit Panjang LRT Line opens.
  • 18 Jan 2003: Sengkang LRT Line (East Loop) opens. West Loop opened on 29 Jan 2005.
  • 20 Jun 2003: North East Line opens.
  • 29 Jan 2005: Punggol LRT Line (East Loop) opens. West loop opened on 29 Jun 2014
  • 28 May 2009: Circle Line opens from Bartley to Marymount. Then on 17 Apr 2010, Circle Line opens from Bartley to Dhoby Ghaut after delays due to the Nicoll Highway collapse.
  • 8 Oct 2011: Circle Line fully opens.

Making alternative routes available

  • 22 Dec 2013: Downtown Line opens from Chinatown to Bugis. Then on 27 Dec 2015, Downtown Line opens from Bugis to Expo.
  • 21 Oct 2017: Downtown Line fully opens.
  • 21 Jan 2020: Thomson-East Coast Line opens from Woodlands North to Woodlands South.
  • 23 Jun 2024: Thomson-East Coast Line fully opens.

Public Buses in Singapore

Introduction

The history of buses in Singapore dates back to the 1920s, when the Singapore Traction Company (STC) began operating bus services. Over the decades, the bus network expanded significantly, becoming a crucial part of Singapore's public transport system.

Now, 3.6 million rides taken per day on average as of Dec 2021, with over 300 scheduled bus services and over 100 short-trip variants operated by 4 different bus operators. In total, there are 5800 buses in operation as of 2024.

Key Milestones

Humble beginnings

  • 1925: The Singapore Traction Company begins operating trolleybus services to replace the ill-fated tram system.
  • 1927: Many former "mosquito-buses" (a informal form of "paratransit") are formalized into the bus network, that connected rural parts of Singapore to the town.
  • 1941: During the Japanese Occupation during WWII, all bus operations were briefly unified under a single operator.

Post-war troubles

  • 1945: STC slowly return to normal operations with its older fleet replaced by newer buses from the UK.
  • 1950s: General discontent on the poor working conditions of the employees working for STC lead to the Hock Lee bus riots in 1955. Which lead to the decline of the STC.
  • 1955: Other Chinese bus companies established their presence in the city with replacement services. Illegal "pirate taxis" also flourished during this time, offering a form of relief for stranded commuters.

Government interference

  • 1971: 10 Chinese bus companies merged to form Amalgamated Bus Company, Associated Bus Services and United Bus Company.
  • 1973: The government urged the 3 companies to merge, leading to the formation of the Singapore Bus Services (SBS).
  • 1974: The government helped to overhaul management, work culture and the fleet of SBS. The introduction of priority bus lanes also alleviated some burden from SBS.
  • 1977: SBS introduced the first Double-Decker Bus (the Leyland Atlantean).

The 1980s and 1990s

  • 1982: There were growing concerns that SBS might turn complacent, given that it had a monopoly on bus services in Singapore.
    As such, the government set-up a new operator, Trans-Island Bus Services (TIBS), to compete with SBS.
  • 1987: When the MRT opened, many services were rationalised such that buses complimented the MRT by "feeding" passengers to the MRT stations. Magnetic stored value cards (as used on the MRT) were also implemented across the entire bus network to streamline fare collection.
  • 1993: SBS introduced its first Air-Conditioned Double Decker bus (Leyland Olympian), and three years later, TIBS introduced Singapore's first bendy buses (Mercedes O405G)
  • 1996: SBS had expanded beyond bus operations including taxis (CityCab) and engineering works (SBS Engineering). Hence, SBS Limited has rebranded to DelGro Corporation, with it's bus service subsidary still retaining the name SBS.
  • 1999: SBS won a tender to operate the North East Line, hence, SBS rebranded to SBS Transit (SBST) to reflect it's multimodal services.

The 2000s and 2010s

  • 2004: TIBS was merged into SMRT Corporation. TIBS was rebranded as SMRT Buses.
  • 2006-2009: SBS Transit renewed it's fleet and introduced the first Euro 3 compliant bus and the first Wheelchair Accessible bus in Singapore, the Volvo B9TL. And in 2009, SBST and SMRT Buses introduced low-entry Scania K230UB and Mercedes OC500LE buses respectively.
  • 2009: LTA became the bus network planner, to work with commuters and the bus operators to improve the overall bus service.
  • 2010: LTA introduced the Bus Service Enhancement Programme (BSEP) to improve bus services, by increasing the number of buses on the road. With 1000 government-funded buses added to the fleet, the initiative aimed to reduce waiting times and enhance service reliability.

2010s: The Government Steps in once again

  • 2014: LTA introduced the Bus Contracting Model (BCM) from 2016 onwards. Under this model, bus operators would bid for contracts to operate specific bus routes, with the government retaining ownership of the bus assets.
  • 2016: The Bus Contracting Model brought in 2 new operators, Go-Ahead Singapore and Tower Transit Singapore, to operate bus services in Singapore.
  • 2020: By then, many old buses (that did not meet the Euro III emmission standards) were retired. A Mercedes-Benz O405G plated TIB1238H has been preserved in Woodlands Bus Depot.

Fun Facts!

  • Did you know? At City Hall and Raffles Place stations, you can transfer almost seamlessly by walking across the platforms between East West Line and North South Line. This is called a "Cross Platform Transfer"
  • There are also connecting tracks between the East West Line and North South Line at City Hall and Raffles Place.
  • Did you know? North East Line is the world's first fully underground automated railway. Its also the only line to use a 1500V DC overhead catenary (wires) on the MRT System. Its also the first line to have artworks displayed at all its 17 stations.
  • Did you know? During the construction of the North East Line, workers building Punggol MRT station had to take a shuttle to the construction site as the area back then was just jungle? There were many encounters with wildlife during the construction.
  • Did you know? There was a MRT Line originally planned to serve the Eastern part of Singapore? Now it's part of the Downtown Line (eastern section) and the Thomson-East Coast Line (East Coast section)
  • Did you know? Back when the Changi Airport Branch opened, trains from Changi Airport travelled all the way to Boon Lay (East West Line's then terminus). But due to the lack of demand, it was shortened to terminate at Tanah Merah, operating a shuttle like service.
  • Did you know? Shenton Way Bus Terminal is not near Shenton Way MRT station? This is because the Bus Terminal was built before the MRT station.
  • Did you know? There used to be Premium bus services operated by SBS Transit? Now they are operated under private bus companies as scheme B bus services. LTA has not given any additional information about it
  • Did you know? SBS Transit used to brand their express services (services with the suffix 'e') as "Fast Forward"
  • Did you know? It was Trans-Island Bus Services that first started using electronic display systems (The display that shows service destination and number)
  • Did you know? Some retired buses can get sold to other countries? One example is the old SBS Transit Mercedes O405 buses exported to Thailand where they still run today!
  • Did you know? Many bus service numbers are given according to where they ply?
